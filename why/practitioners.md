# Practitioner characteristics

Understanding why technical adoption is a learning and trust challenge — not a marketing one — begins with understanding the people at the centre of it.

The practitioners DevRel works with tend to share a recognisable profile. This profile was first described in the context of gifted learners ([Mark Herrera](https://www.linkedin.com/in/mark-anthony-herrera-337259225/) / [Steve Halladay](https://www.linkedin.com/in/steve-halladay-63b1441/)), but it maps closely to how experienced technical practitioners approach new knowledge.

---

## How practitioners learn

**They go deep on what interests them.** Practitioners do not skim. When something catches their attention, they pursue it with intensity — persistent, goal-oriented, with longer attention spans for topics they have chosen. They have high language facility. They read widely and process quickly.

**They are self-directed.** Practitioners seek out their own interests and want to learn at their own pace. They resist being told what to care about. Content that feels structured for a sales process rather than their actual questions is content they stop reading.

**They think at scale.** Practitioners process and retain large amounts of information. They see unusual relationships across disciplines. They transfer knowledge between contexts — not just answering today's question but building a mental model of the whole system.

**They prefer complexity and freedom.** Practitioners prefer challenging work. They generate original ideas and solutions, notice patterns, and form their own ways of thinking about problems. They want freedom in how they learn and space to experiment. Scaffolding that holds them too long or treats them as beginners when they are not loses them.

---

## Why this matters for DevRel

This profile explains why adoption is fundamentally a learning and trust challenge, not a marketing one.

Practitioners cannot be persuaded to adopt something by repeating its features. They will evaluate it themselves, on their own terms, against their own criteria. They will look for peers who have done it. They will probe the edges before they trust the centre. They will share what they find — honestly — with the people around them.

DevRel's job is to meet practitioners in that process. To create the conditions in which they can explore, understand, and build confidence — at their pace, not the vendor's. And to hear what they discover, because what practitioners find out independently is often more revealing than anything a structured interview surfaces.

Understanding this profile is the foundation of the [STACK](../how/README.md) framework: why Tutoring must follow the practitioner's pace, why Autonomy is a goal rather than an afterthought, and why Kinship — practitioners learning alongside each other — is often more effective than anything a DevRel team can deliver directly.
