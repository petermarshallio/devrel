# Why DevRel exists

DevRel sits between two sets of goals that are related but not identical — and its job is to hold both simultaneously without betraying either.

The organisation has goals. The ecosystem has goals. When those goals align, DevRel accelerates that alignment. When they diverge, DevRel surfaces the tension honestly — to both sides. The function that only serves the organisation's goals is a marketing unit. The function that only advocates for practitioners has no mandate. DevRel's value comes precisely from holding both.

---

## The graph model

Developer ecosystems are graphs.

The **nodes** are the people and organisations in the ecosystem: practitioners building with the technology, companies depending on it, contributors shaping it, vendors investing in it. Every node has a position in the graph — how many connections it has, how strong those connections are, whether it sits in a densely connected cluster or in relative isolation.

The **edges** are what connect the nodes: relationships, knowledge flows, dependencies, trust, shared history, mutual obligation. An edge between two practitioners might be built on a conference hallway conversation, a forum thread where one answered the other's question at 2am, or years of co-authoring integrations. Some edges are strong — load-bearing, reciprocal, durable. Some are weak — directional, transactional, fragile. Both matter.

The graph has topology. At early maturity, it resembles a star: everything routes through a central node (usually the vendor or DevRel). As the ecosystem develops, direct practitioner-to-practitioner edges multiply. The graph becomes denser, more resilient, more distributed. Knowledge circulates without requiring a central router. This progression — from star to mesh — is what ecosystem maturity describes.

**DevRel's job is to see the graph and tend it.** Not to control it. Not to sit permanently at its centre. To understand the topology, identify where nodes are isolated or edges are weak, and create the conditions under which the graph can strengthen itself. The DevRel function that has done its job well is no longer the hub — the ecosystem has enough direct connections to operate without routing everything through it.

## The value exchange

The graph is not a static structure. Value flows along its edges constantly, in both directions.

### What flows from the organisation to the ecosystem

The organisation brings things the ecosystem needs:

- **Knowledge** — documentation, education, tutorials, reference material; the content that reduces the cognitive cost of adoption
- **Tools** — SDKs, APIs, CLIs, extension frameworks; the infrastructure that makes it possible to build
- **Access** — roadmap visibility, early previews, design partnerships, product team time; the mechanisms that make the ecosystem's voice consequential
- **Recognition** — Guardian programmes, speaker platforms, co-authorship, public acknowledgement; the signals that tell practitioners their contribution is seen and valued
- **Trust** — the consistent, non-transactional relationship that makes a practitioner believe the organisation is worth investing in

When the organisation invests in these things, it is not being generous. It is building the conditions under which the ecosystem can grow in ways that serve the organisation's goals. Knowledge reduces support load and accelerates adoption. Tools multiply what practitioners can build. Access produces the feedback that shapes better product decisions. Recognition identifies and retains the practitioners who are structurally most important to ecosystem health.

### What flows from the ecosystem to the organisation

The ecosystem gives back things the organisation needs:

- **Signal** — the honest, unfiltered view of what works, what does not, what is missing, and what the next problem will be; the feedback that does not appear in support tickets or structured product interviews
- **Capability** — contributions, integrations, extensions, content, and co-created architectures that multiply the technology's reach beyond what the internal team can build
- **Advocacy** — word of mouth, practitioner testimonials, conference talks, blog posts, and the quiet recommendation one engineer makes to another that is worth more than any advertisement
- **Talent** — practitioners who understand the technology deeply and whose next career move is toward the organisation, not away from it
- **Resilience** — the distributed knowledge and mutual-aid capacity of a healthy ecosystem, which means the organisation is not the sole load-bearing entity when things break

These flows are not guaranteed. They are the product of a healthy value exchange. An ecosystem that receives knowledge and tools but experiences no genuine access, recognition, or trust will extract what it needs and give back little. The practitioner who does not believe the organisation is listening has no incentive to keep speaking.

### DevRel as the managed interface

DevRel is the function that manages both directions of this exchange deliberately.

It is not the only interface between the organisation and the ecosystem — Engineering, Product, Sales, and Customer Success all have their own touchpoints. But DevRel is the function whose primary mandate is the health of the exchange itself, not any single transaction within it. Sales is optimising for a deal. Marketing is optimising for reach. DevRel is optimising for the quality of the relationship over time.

That distinction gives DevRel a position no other function holds: trusted by practitioners in ways that sales and marketing are not, and mandated by the organisation in ways that community volunteers and external advocates are not. It can carry information in both directions that would otherwise not flow — because the practitioner trusts the person, and the organisation has authorised the access.

In Porter's value chain terms, DevRel is neither purely a primary activity nor purely a support function — it is the hub between them. Sales, Marketing, and Customer Success are primary activities; Engineering and Product are support functions. DevRel sits across that boundary deliberately: it opens the door between Engineering and the practitioners who depend on what Engineering builds; it connects what Marketing is saying to what practitioners are actually experiencing; it gives Customer Success the practitioner context that makes commercial relationships more honest. Not a controller of those flows — a facilitator. Not the only route, but often the most trusted one.

## Two sets of goals

The value exchange works when the organisation's goals and the ecosystem's goals are both being served. DevRel's job is to understand both clearly — and to design programmes, relationships, and interventions that operate in the alignment zone as much as possible while managing the tension zones honestly.

### Our goals

The organisation's goals in the ecosystem are typically:

- **Adoption** — practitioners choosing this technology over alternatives, and using it in production
- **Retention** — practitioners staying, deepening their investment, not churning silently
- **Market awareness** — the technology being known, understood, and considered by the right practitioners in the right contexts
- **Product intelligence** — understanding what practitioners actually need, what is broken, what the roadmap should prioritise
- **Ecosystem leverage** — the ecosystem contributing capability, content, and advocacy that multiplies what the internal team can produce
- **Revenue** — either directly (commercial products, certifications, support contracts) or indirectly (ecosystem health as a condition for commercial success)

These are legitimate organisational goals. DevRel is not embarrassed by them. But pursuing them clumsily — treating practitioners as targets rather than participants, measuring relationships in pipeline rather than trust, demanding advocacy without investing in the conditions that make advocacy genuine — destroys the ecosystem's willingness to participate in the exchange.

### Their goals

The ecosystem's goals are different in character:

- **Solve real problems** — practitioners want to build things that work; everything else is secondary
- **Build capability and confidence** — they want to become more skilled, more effective, more valuable; they are not primarily interested in becoming expert in any specific vendor's technology unless that expertise serves their broader development
- **Be genuinely heard** — particularly the Guardians and contributors who invest significantly in the ecosystem; they want their experience and their feedback to have visible consequences, not to disappear into a survey results summary
- **Belong to something real** — the kinship of shared technical struggle is a genuine human need; practitioners want to be part of an ecosystem that has texture and fellowship, not just a user base
- **Not be used** — practitioners are technically sophisticated and pattern-match quickly to transactional intent; the organisation that treats them as a marketing channel will lose their trust faster than it gained it
- **Advance their own work and reputation** — participation in the ecosystem should serve the practitioner's career and technical goals, not just the vendor's commercial ones

### Alignment zones

Some activities naturally serve both sets of goals simultaneously. These are the most productive DevRel investments:

- **Education and documentation** — the organisation needs practitioners to adopt; practitioners need to understand the technology. Knowledge transfer serves both.
- **Honest feedback loops** — the organisation needs product intelligence; practitioners need to believe their voice matters. A genuine feedback mechanism serves both — but only if the organisation actually listens and responds.
- **Guardian programmes** — the organisation needs resilient, distributed ecosystem capability; practitioners who are load-bearing in the ecosystem need recognition, access, and the sense that their investment is honoured. A well-run Guardian programme serves both.
- **Practitioner storytelling** — the organisation needs market credibility; practitioners who have done interesting things want a platform and an audience. A co-produced story can serve both — but only if the practitioner controls the narrative and the audience is other practitioners, not buyers.

### Tension zones

Some activities create tension between the two sets of goals. These require honesty and care, not avoidance:

- **Advocacy pressure** — the organisation wants practitioners to recommend the technology; practitioners will recommend it if it is good and if they trust the relationship, but will resist if asked to perform advocacy they do not feel. The tension is resolved by earning advocacy, not requesting it.
- **Access as leverage** — roadmap sessions and early access are genuine benefits DevRel can offer Guardians; but if access is made explicitly conditional on advocacy, it poisons the relationship. Access should be given because practitioners' input is genuinely valued, not as payment for promotion.
- **Measurement** — the organisation needs to demonstrate DevRel's value; practitioners do not want to be reduced to metrics or to feel their relationship is being tracked for reporting purposes. The tension is managed by measuring ecosystem health signals rather than relationship transactions.
- **Commercial context** — a practitioner who is also a customer exists in two relationships simultaneously: the practitioner relationship (non-transactional, DevRel-mediated) and the commercial relationship (transactional, Sales/CS-mediated). Conflating them destroys the former. DevRel needs to be clear internally about which relationship it holds and protect the boundary.

## Donors, Beneficiaries, and Guardians

The value exchange produces three distinct participant types. Understanding where each sits in the exchange — and what they need — shapes how DevRel tends the graph.

**Beneficiaries** are receiving from the ecosystem. Knowledge, tools, answers, confidence. Every practitioner starts here. The Beneficiary's primary goal is to solve their problem and build their capability. The organisation's goal is for them to adopt successfully, build real things, and not churn. The alignment zone is wide: good Information programmes serve both simultaneously.

**Donors** are contributing to the ecosystem without a direct return on any specific contribution. They write the tutorial, answer the forum question, speak at the meetup. Their goals include reputation, craft, and genuine care for the ecosystem they have benefited from. The organisation's goal is for their contribution to be excellent and for the ecosystem to retain them. The alignment zone requires the organisation to be a good steward: recognising contribution, not exploiting it, and ensuring that Donors' work is visible and valued.

**Guardians** are doing both — and are structurally load-bearing as a result. Like a keystone species in an ecological sense, they are disproportionately important to the health of the whole — remove them, and the ecosystem restructures around the gap in ways that are hard to predict and harder to undo. A Guardian who leaves takes a disproportionate number of edges with them. Their presence makes the ecosystem resilient; their absence creates gaps. The organisation's goal is to help Guardians emerge and to retain them. The Guardian's goal is to be genuinely invested in and genuinely heard — not just celebrated. The tension zone here is real: the organisation that treats Guardians as unpaid marketing resources will lose them. The organisation that treats them as partners in the ecosystem's health will keep them.

The Beneficiary → Guardian transition is not automatic. It requires the ecosystem to give enough, and for the practitioner to care enough about the ecosystem to give back. DevRel's role is to create the conditions that make that transition feel natural — that make contributing feel worthwhile, that make the Guardian's investment feel consequential. The moment a practitioner answers their first question in a community channel, or files their first substantive bug report, or helps onboard the person sitting next to them at a hackathon — that is the transition. DevRel's job is to make sure that moment is noticed, rewarded, and likely to be repeated.

## What happens when the exchange breaks down

The value exchange can fail in two directions.

**The organisation extracts without investing.** Knowledge flows from practitioners to the organisation (feedback, signal, advocacy) without a commensurate flow of access, recognition, or genuine hearing in return. Practitioners notice. The Guardians — the ones with the most to give and the most to lose — disengage first. The ecosystem becomes shallower. The signal dries up. The advocacy becomes hollow. What looks like a healthy user base is in fact a set of Beneficiaries who will move on as soon as a better option appears.

**The organisation invests without purpose.** Resources flow outward — events, content, education — without a coherent understanding of what the organisation needs from the ecosystem and how those investments connect to it. DevRel becomes an activity function: doing things that look like DevRel without a clear theory of how they change the graph. The investment is real; the return is unmeasurable; the function is defunded.

The healthy exchange is intentional in both directions. The organisation knows what it needs from the ecosystem and designs programmes that earn it — by genuinely serving the ecosystem's goals first. DevRel is the function that holds that intention, manages the interface, and keeps both sides of the exchange honest.
