# Why DevRel?

> Developer Relations is an organisational capability for reducing the uncertainty, friction, and cognitive cost of technical adoption while increasing practitioner capability, confidence, autonomy, and kinship.

That definition rewards slow reading. Each word is load-bearing. Let's unpack it.

---

## The graph model

Developer ecosystems are graphs.

The nodes are people and organisations: practitioners building with your technology, companies depending on it, contributors shaping it. The edges are the relationships, collaborations, dependencies, and exchanges between them — trust formed over time, knowledge shared across a forum post, a dependency adopted because someone vouched for it, a feature built because someone filed a well-articulated issue.

DevRel's job is to see that graph and tend it.

Not to market. Not to sell. To survey the topology — identifying isolated nodes, weak edges, and structural gaps — and to intervene thoughtfully: strengthening connections, creating new ones, reducing friction, building the conditions under which the graph can operate without everything routing through DevRel itself.

Developer ecosystems are socio-technical systems. Adoption is not primarily a marketing challenge. It is a learning and trust challenge. Developers need to understand something before they can use it. They need to trust it before they will depend on it. They need to be confident in it before they will advocate for it. DevRel exists at exactly that intersection — where knowledge, trust, and confidence either compound or collapse.

---

## The value exchange

Three types of participant exist in any healthy ecosystem.

**Donors** contribute to the ecosystem — code, knowledge, feedback, advocacy, time. They give more than they take in the short term. They write the blog post, answer the forum question, speak at the conference.

**Beneficiaries** receive value from the ecosystem — capability, confidence, solutions, shortcuts. They are the practitioners who find what they need, use it well, and get on with their work.

**Guardians** are both simultaneously. A Guardian is a practitioner who has received enough from the ecosystem to become structurally load-bearing within it: they answer questions, mentor others, contribute back, and carry technical knowledge that others depend on. Like a keystone species in an ecological sense — disproportionately important to the health of the whole. Guardians are also very sticky. Their investment in the ecosystem deepens their attachment to it.

DevRel cultivates conditions for all three. But it pays particular attention to helping Guardians emerge. A practitioner who moves from Beneficiary to Guardian is not just a happy user — they are a node with strong bidirectional edges, a multiplier in the graph, a signal of ecosystem health that no other metric captures as well.

The goal is not advocacy. The goal is the conditions under which Guardians naturally arise.

## The maturity model

Ecosystems mature as graphs. The progression is one of topology:

**1. Isolated** — disconnected nodes; high dependency on the vendor to route everything. Practitioners cannot find each other. Knowledge does not circulate. Every question comes back to the source.

**2. Dependent** — a star topology begins to form. Practitioners find DevRel as a hub. Connections are present but all routes pass through the centre. The ecosystem is functional but fragile.

**3. Contributing** — direct node-to-node edges begin to appear. Practitioners start answering each other's questions. Code contributions emerge. The graph has density it didn't before.

**4. Participating** — rich graph topology. Many direct connections. Knowledge circulates without DevRel routing it. Guardians are visible and active. The ecosystem has operational resilience.

**5. Self-organising** — a resilient mesh. The ecosystem operates, adapts, and grows largely independently. DevRel's role shifts from tending to observing, from routing to sensing. The graph sustains itself.

This is ecosystem operational maturity — not community engagement maturity. A mature ecosystem is not one with high follower counts or event attendance. It is one that is operationally capable, collaboratively resilient, and increasingly self-organising.

## Signals

Healthy ecosystems are observable. DevRel is the function that instruments that observability.

The sector has long confused metrics with signals. Metrics count things — registrations, page views, event attendance, GitHub stars. Signals reveal things — where the friction is, which nodes are isolated, where trust has formed, where dependency is unhealthy, who is becoming a Guardian.

Intel is the observability layer that runs beneath all DevRel activity. It is not a programme or a deliverable. It is a discipline of noticing. DevRel practitioners — because of their proximity to practitioners in honest, non-transactional contexts — surface signals that no other function sees. The product team hears what customers say in structured interviews. DevRel hears what practitioners say when they don't know they're being heard.

That intelligence, fed back into product, sales, customer success, and leadership, is one of DevRel's most strategically valuable outputs. Not because the data is clean, but because it is real.

## Why organisations need DevRel

> Use metrics to clarify areas of uncertainty. Consider the risk to the business of inaction, not just of action.
>
> [Myrsini Koukiasa](https://www.linkedin.com/in/myrsinikoukiasa/), DevRelCon London, 2023

> DevRel and growth is the bridge between developers and sales, between developers, sales and product.
>
> [Daniel Bryant](https://developerrelations.com/strategy-and-metrics/is-product-led-growth-the-devops-of-the-devrel-world)

DevRel is organisational observability for technical adoption.

Other functions see parts of the graph. Sales sees the deals. Marketing sees the reach. Product sees the feature requests. Engineering sees the codebase. DevRel sees the practitioners — how they think, what they struggle with, what they trust, what they tell each other.

In Porter's value chain terms, DevRel is neither a primary nor a support function in isolation. It is a hub between them — not a controller, but a facilitator. It opens doors. It brings people together across the boundaries where knowledge and trust would otherwise not flow. It reduces the organisational friction that makes adoption harder than it needs to be.

The question is not whether your practitioners need help. They do. The question is whether your organisation can afford not to see the graph.
