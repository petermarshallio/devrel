# Signals

> Be specific. Be focused on what needs to be solved. Use metrics to assess tolerance for levels of risk.
>
> [Myrsini Koukiasa](https://www.linkedin.com/in/myrsinikoukiasa/), DevRelCon London, 2023

The word "metrics" in DevRel has a complicated history. Too often it has meant counting things that are easy to count — page views, event attendees, social media impressions — and calling that measurement. Those numbers are not wrong. They are just not very revealing.

The framework here treats them as signals: telemetry that, read correctly, reveals something true about the state of the ecosystem. Whether the graph is becoming more connected or more isolated. Where friction lives. Which nodes are load-bearing. Where trust is forming or fraying.

---

## Reading signals

> If the data shows a spike, it's a technical change. If it's a slope, it's a social / people change. Be the developer. But always verify the data — be the developer and experience it for yourself.
>
> [Richard Millington](https://www.linkedin.com/in/richard-millington-5a32782/), DevRelCon London 2023

Millington's distinction is operationally important. A spike in forum activity after a release is a technical signal — something changed in the product. A slow decline in forum participation over six months is a social signal — something is changing in the ecosystem. The response to each is different. DevRel practitioners who conflate the two will misread both.

Signals require interpretation, not just collection. The Intel discipline is not about dashboards. It is about practitioners who are close enough to the ecosystem — through non-transactional relationships and genuine presence — to know what a number means.

## Four types of ecosystem signal

**Ecosystem health signals** — Is the graph becoming more connected or more isolated? Are Guardians emerging or atrophying? Are practitioners finding each other without DevRel as the route?

Indicators: peer-to-peer question answering, unsolicited contributions, cross-organisation collaboration, event attendance that isn't vendor-driven.

**Friction signals** — Where are practitioners getting stuck? What questions keep recurring? What does the support queue reveal about documentation gaps? What is the gap between what practitioners attempt and what they achieve?

Indicators: recurring questions in forums, high drop-off points in tutorials, support volume by topic, time-to-first-working-implementation.

**Dependency signals** — Which nodes are load-bearing? What would happen if they left? Which accounts route all their technical questions through one person? Which Guardians are showing signs of fatigue?

Indicators: concentration of answers from single contributors, practitioner tenure, account-level single points of failure, burnout signals in high-frequency contributors.

**Emerging Guardian signals** — Which practitioners are starting to give back? Who answered a question without being asked? Who filed a detailed bug report rather than just abandoning the product? Who showed up to the hackathon and then came back?

Indicators: first contribution events, transition from question-asker to question-answerer, voluntary content creation, informal mentoring visible in public threads.

## Signals by programme family

Each programme family generates its own signal profile:

| Programme family | What it surfaces |
| --- | --- |
| Information | Friction in adoption; documentation gaps; time-to-competence |
| Inspiration | Reach and resonance of content; share of voice in the ecosystem |
| Involvement | Depth and density of participation; Beneficiary-to-Donor transitions |
| Innovation | Quality of product feedback; roadmap signal strength; practitioner capability at the edge |

Intel sits beneath all four, synthesising signals across them into a coherent picture of ecosystem health.

---

## How signals route

Different signals are valuable to different parts of the organisation. DevRel's role is not just to collect signals but to route them:

| Signal type | Where it routes |
| --- | --- |
| Ecosystem health | Leadership, Product |
| Friction | Product, Docs, Customer Success |
| Dependency | Sales, Customer Success, Leadership |
| Emerging Guardian | DevRel (internal), Customer Success |
| Adoption velocity | Sales, Marketing |
| Content resonance | Marketing |
| Roadmap signal | Product, R&D |

The routing is as important as the collection. A friction signal that stays inside DevRel and never reaches the documentation team has not done its job.
