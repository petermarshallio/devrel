# Expert Educator

**Instrument:** Logos — knowledge and evidence **Archetype:** David Attenborough
## What they do

The Expert Educator moves people up Bloom's taxonomy: from awareness through
understanding to application, analysis, synthesis, and evaluation. Knowledge
flows outward. Their work is the systematic reduction of the distance between
what a practitioner does not know and what they need to know to be effective.

This sounds simple. It is not. The EE must hold two things simultaneously: deep
technical or conceptual expertise, and the ability to translate that expertise
into something a learner at a different level can absorb. The translation is the
craft. Getting it wrong in either direction — too shallow, or too dense — means
the knowledge does not land.

The EE is not just a teacher in the classroom sense. They are the person who
writes the tutorial that becomes the reference, records the deep-dive that
explains not just what the system does but why it was designed that way, builds
the course that scaffolds a practitioner from zero to production, answers the
forum question in a way that helps not just the asker but the next hundred
people who find it by search.
## What they look like in practice

The EE's output is visible and durable. Tutorials, reference documentation,
courses, notebooks, explainer videos, conference talks with real technical
depth, written guides that practitioners bookmark and return to.

They are often the people in the ecosystem who are cited. Not just quoted —
cited. "Have you read [person]'s post on X?" is the EE's characteristic form of
reputation.

They may be extroverts who own a stage, or introverts who prefer to write. The
mode of expression varies; the instrument is the same. David Attenborough does
not need to be in the scene with the animals — his knowledge and the quality of
his explanation are what create the experience.
## Where they come from

EEs most often arrive in DevRel from engineering, pre-sales, solutions
architecture, or curriculum design. They have spent years getting genuinely good
at something, and at some point realised that explaining it well was as
satisfying as doing it — sometimes more.

The technical credibility is load-bearing. An EE without genuine expertise is
not an EE — they are a writer or presenter, which are different and valuable
roles, but not this one.
## Where they thrive in the developer journey

The EE is most naturally at home in the pre-production stages of the developer
journey — from the moment a practitioner first encounters a technology through
to their first production deployment. This maps to the Plan, Code, Build, and
Test phases of the development cycle.

This is where the questions are most structured, the knowledge gaps are most
predictable, and a well-designed educational asset can serve hundreds of
practitioners at once. The EE's work scales in a way that relationship-based
work does not.

They are also valuable at the Advocacy stage — training and equipping the
Guardians who will go on to teach others.
## What they need to develop

The EE's development challenge is almost never technical depth — it is reach and
accessibility.

Specifically:

- **Communication craft** — the ability to calibrate the level of a piece to its
  audience, to know when an analogy will help and when it will mislead, to
  structure a complex topic so that each piece of understanding builds the next
- **Audience awareness** — understanding that the practitioner reading the
  tutorial at 11pm is not in the same state as the one attending the workshop on
  a Tuesday morning, and designing for both
- **Measurement instinct** — knowing whether the content is actually landing:
  completion rates, return visits, whether the questions downstream change after
  a piece is published

The EE who develops these things becomes genuinely rare. Technical depth is more
common than it appears. The combination of depth and the ability to transfer it
is not.
