# Innovation

**Strategic goal:** [Product development](../goals/product-development.md)
**Journey stages:** [Application](../journey/application.md) → [Advocacy](../journey/advocacy.md)
**Primary practitioner:** [Connected Correspondent](../practitioners/connected-correspondent.md)

---

Innovation programmes close the loop. The practitioner who has reached Application — who is running the technology at scale, hitting the real edges, forming the real opinions — holds knowledge the product team cannot manufacture. They know which parts of the documentation lie. They know which architectural pattern everyone uses and which one the docs recommend. They know what the technology cannot yet do that they need it to do.

Innovation programmes are how that knowledge reaches the people who can act on it.

This is not a feedback inbox. It is a relationship-mediated signal channel — one that only works because the practitioner trusts the person on the other end. The Connected Correspondent's value here is precisely that they are not a product manager, not a salesperson, and not an account manager. They are a peer. The unfiltered story that never makes it into a support ticket or a sales call has a way of coming out in that conversation.

Innovation also operates in the other direction. Practitioners who contribute to the product — who help shape the roadmap, who validate designs before they ship, who build the integrations that extend the technology's reach — develop a qualitatively different stake in its success. The practitioner who helped design a feature does not churn quietly. They are, by definition, a Guardian.

## Programmes

### Feedback

The systematic practice of surfacing practitioner experience — friction, unmet needs, workarounds, opinions — and routing it to the teams that can act on it.

Feedback is not a survey. A survey tells you what practitioners are willing to say in a structured format, under their own name, to a vendor. That is useful, but it is the curated version. The unfiltered version — the thing a practitioner says to someone they trust, not to someone with a quota — is qualitatively different. The Connected Correspondent is the person who gets that version.

| Dimension | Description |
| --- | --- |
| **People** | Led by CC practitioners; EE practitioners contribute technical synthesis (translating practitioner experience into product-ready signal); partnered with Product (routing and prioritisation), Customer Success (commercial signal context), and Sales (account-specific intelligence) |
| **Process** | Relationship maintenance → active listening (in community channels, at events, in 1:1 conversations) → signal capture → synthesis → routing to Product and Customer Success → closed-loop acknowledgement back to the practitioner |
| **Place** | 1:1 conversations at events and online (synchronous); community channel monitoring (asynchronous); practitioner interviews (synchronous); private channels with key Guardians (asynchronous) |
| **Technology** | Community analytics platforms for signal detection; CRM for relationship and signal tracking; AI-assisted synthesis of community sentiment and recurring themes; tagging and routing workflows into product management tooling |

**Signal:** Volume and specificity of actionable feedback routed to Product; ratio of CC-sourced feedback to support-ticket-sourced feedback; practitioner-reported sense that their voice reaches the roadmap; feature adoption rate for features developed with practitioner input.

### Roadmap sessions

The structured access programme through which key practitioners — primarily Guardians — have direct input to product direction. This is the formal seat at the table: early previews of the roadmap, the chance to validate assumptions before they become commitments, and the relationship with the product team that makes the feedback real rather than notional.

Roadmap sessions are not the same as beta programmes or early access. They are not about testing a finished feature — they are about shaping a feature before it is finished. The distinction matters to the practitioner: being invited to a roadmap session signals that their opinion is valued before decisions are made, not after.

| Dimension | Description |
| --- | --- |
| **People** | Led by CC practitioners (who hold the Guardian relationships); Product leads the content; EE practitioners contribute where technical depth is needed to have the conversation; partnered with Engineering for deep-dive sessions |
| **Process** | Guardian identification → invitation and expectation-setting → roadmap briefing → structured feedback capture → synthesis and routing back to Product → closed-loop update to participants on what was heard and what changed |
| **Place** | Private video sessions with product team (synchronous); in-person roundtables at major events (synchronous); asynchronous review of pre-release documentation or design proposals |
| **Technology** | Secure document sharing for NDA-protected roadmap content; structured feedback tooling (forms, comment platforms); AI-assisted synthesis of session output; CRM tracking of who has been briefed on what and when |

**Signal:** Guardian participation rate in roadmap sessions; product changes directly attributable to Guardian input; Guardian retention rate (Guardians who have been to a roadmap session versus those who have not); practitioner NPS among roadmap session participants.

### Design partnerships

The deepest form of Innovation programme — a structured co-creation relationship with a small number of practitioners who are building something meaningful with the technology and willing to build it alongside the product team.

A design partnership is not a beta. A beta is a finished feature given to many practitioners for validation. A design partnership is a feature in formation given to a small number of practitioners for shaping. The practitioner contributes their real requirements, their architectural context, and their willingness to try things that do not work yet. The organisation contributes early access, direct product team attention, and the genuine possibility of changing direction based on what is learned.

Design partnerships are expensive in relationship capital. They only work if the organisation genuinely listens — if the practitioner's experience has visible consequences for what ships. The CC who runs a design partnership and sees nothing change will struggle to recruit the next one.

| Dimension | Description |
| --- | --- |
| **People** | Led by CC practitioners (relationship trust) and EE practitioners (technical depth for meaningful co-creation); Product and Engineering are primary co-leads; partnered with Customer Success where the practitioner is also a commercial account |
| **Process** | Use-case identification → partner selection → expectation-setting (what influence is realistic) → iterative co-design sessions → feedback synthesis → product decision (with explanation back to partners) → post-launch follow-up |
| **Place** | Regular working sessions (synchronous, video or in-person); shared working environments (staging environments, pre-release docs, private repos) — asynchronous; occasional in-person deep-dives at product or partner offices |
| **Technology** | Shared pre-release environments; private GitHub repositories or issue trackers for co-development; secure communication channels; AI-assisted analysis of usage patterns from partner environments to accelerate feedback cycles |

**Signal:** Features shipped with direct design partner input; partner satisfaction with influence on final product; post-launch adoption rate among design partner practitioners versus general population; Guardian programme conversion rate from design partnership participants.

## Practitioner fit

Innovation is the natural home of the Connected Correspondent. The trust that makes a practitioner say what they actually think — rather than what they think the vendor wants to hear — is CC-mediated. The Guardian who tells a product manager that the roadmap is wrong tells the CC that the roadmap is wrong. The distinction is not accidental.

The EE contributes to Innovation through the technical precision that makes feedback actionable. A CC who surfaces a practitioner's frustration without the technical synthesis to turn it into a clear signal has still done valuable work — but the signal that lands in Product as "practitioners are frustrated with the performance of X in Y workload under Z conditions" is more useful than "practitioners don't like X."

Innovation is where the EE and CC collaborate most naturally. The CC builds the relationship. The EE makes the signal legible.

## Organisational partners

| Partner | Role in Innovation programmes |
| --- | --- |
| **Product** | Primary consumer of Innovation output; owns roadmap decisions; responsible for closed-loop acknowledgement back to practitioners |
| **Engineering** | Deep technical engagement in design partnerships; source of truth for what is and is not buildable within a given timeframe |
| **Customer Success** | Commercial context for Innovation signals; relationship overlap with Guardians who are also commercial customers; partner in identifying which feedback is adoption friction versus genuine product gap |
| **Sales** | Account-specific intelligence that puts product feedback in commercial context; surface introduction opportunities for practitioners willing to participate in Innovation programmes |
