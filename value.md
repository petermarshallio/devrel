# The value exchange

Along the tendrils of the mesh in the community in which DevRel operates are nuggets of value. Like the cells in our blood, they feed and protect the whole body.

- **Knowledge** — documentation, education, tutorials, reference material; the content that reduces the cognitive cost of adoption.
- **Tools** — SDKs, APIs, CLIs, extension frameworks; the infrastructure that makes it possible to build.
- **Access** — roadmap visibility, early previews, design partnerships, product team time; the mechanisms that make the ecosystem's voice consequential.
- **Recognition** — Guardian programmes, speaker platforms, co-authorship, public acknowledgement; the signals that tell practitioners their contribution is seen and valued.
- **Trust** — the consistent, non-transactional relationship that makes a practitioner believe the organisation is worth investing in.

When the organisation invests in these things, it is not being generous. It is building the conditions under which the ecosystem can grow in ways that serve the organisation's goals. Knowledge reduces support load and accelerates adoption. Tools multiply what practitioners can build. Access produces the feedback that shapes better product decisions. Recognition identifies and retains the practitioners who are structurally most important to ecosystem health.

This is all well and Good! But what does a company get back for all their money? The network is a living breathing thing. This organism must be doing something other than just eating lots of pizza...

- **Signal** — the honest, unfiltered view of what works, what does not, what is missing, and what the next problem will be; the feedback that does not appear in support tickets or structured product interviews
- **Capability** — contributions, integrations, extensions, content, and co-created architectures that multiply the technology's reach beyond what the internal team can build
- **Advocacy** — word of mouth, practitioner testimonials, conference talks, blog posts, and the quiet recommendation one engineer makes to another that is worth more than any advertisement
- **Talent** — practitioners who understand the technology deeply and whose next career move is toward the organisation, not away from it
- **Resilience** — the distributed knowledge and mutual-aid capacity of a healthy ecosystem, which means the organisation is not the sole load-bearing entity when things break

These flows are not guaranteed. They are the product of a healthy value exchange put in place and nurtured by DevRel.

An ecosystem that receives knowledge and tools but experiences no genuine access, recognition, or trust will extract what it needs and give back little. The practitioner who does not believe the organisation is listening has no incentive to keep speaking.

DevRel is the _only_ function that deliberately manages both directions of this value exchange into and out of the beast. DevRel is the _only_ function whose primary mandate is the health of the exchange itself, not any single transaction within it. Sales is optimising for a deal. Marketing is optimising for reach.

_DevRel is optimising for the quality of value exchange over time._

That distinction gives DevRel a position no other function holds: trusted by practitioners in ways that sales and marketing are not, and mandated by the organisation in ways that community volunteers and external advocates are not.

It can carry information in both directions that would otherwise not flow — because the practitioner trusts the person, and the organisation has authorised the access.

In terms of Porter's Value Chain, DevRel is _neither_ purely a primary activity _nor_ purely a support function: it is the hub between them.  DevRel sits across that boundary deliberately: it opens the door between Engineering and the practitioners who depend on what Engineering builds; it connects what Marketing is saying to what practitioners are actually experiencing; it gives Customer Success the practitioner context that makes commercial relationships more honest.

DevRel is not a controller of those flows - it is a facilitator. It's not the only route, but it _is_ often the most trusted one.

The organisation's goals in the ecosystem are typically:

- **Adoption** — practitioners choosing this technology over alternatives, and using it in production.
- **Retention** — practitioners staying, deepening their investment, not churning silently.
- **Market awareness** — the technology being known, understood, and considered by the right practitioners in the right contexts.
- **Product intelligence** — understanding what practitioners actually need, what is broken, what the roadmap should prioritise.
- **Ecosystem leverage** — the ecosystem contributing capability, content, and advocacy that multiplies what the internal team can produce.
- **Revenue** — either directly (commercial products, certifications, support contracts) or indirectly (ecosystem health as a condition for commercial success).

And what's wrong with that?! DevRel is not embarrassed by them. But pursuing them clumsily — treating practitioners as targets rather than participants, measuring relationships in pipeline rather than trust, demanding advocacy without investing in the conditions that make advocacy genuine — destroys the ecosystem's willingness to participate in the exchange.

The network's goals are different.

- **Solve real problems** — practitioners want to build things that work; everything else is secondary.
- **Build capability and confidence** — they want to become more skilled, more effective, more valuable; they are not primarily interested in becoming expert in any specific vendor's technology unless that expertise serves their broader development.
- **Be genuinely heard** — particularly the Guardians and contributors who invest significantly in the ecosystem; they want their experience and their feedback to have visible consequences, not to disappear into a survey results summary.
- **Belong to something real** — the kinship of shared technical struggle is a genuine human need; practitioners want to be part of an ecosystem that has texture and fellowship, not just a user base.
- **Not be used** — practitioners are technically sophisticated and pattern-match quickly to transactional intent; the organisation that treats them as a marketing channel will lose their trust faster than it gained it.
- **Advance their own work and reputation** — participation in the ecosystem should serve the practitioner's career and technical goals, not just the vendor's commercial ones.

These are things that we each of us want for our own jobs. There's nothing different here. We are all humans.

> DevRel and growth is the bridge between developers and sales, between developers, sales and product.
> — [Daniel Bryant](https://developerrelations.com/strategy-and-metrics/is-product-led-growth-the-devops-of-the-devrel-world)

Which is what makes DevRel about the humans. And about the ties that bind.

- **Education and documentation** — the organisation needs practitioners to adopt; practitioners need to understand the technology. Knowledge transfer serves both.
- **Honest feedback loops** — the organisation needs product intelligence; practitioners need to believe their voice matters. A genuine feedback mechanism serves both — but only if the organisation actually listens and responds.
- **Guardian programmes** — the organisation needs resilient, distributed ecosystem capability; practitioners who are load-bearing in the ecosystem need recognition, access, and the sense that their investment is honoured. A well-run Guardian programme serves both.
- **Practitioner storytelling** — the organisation needs market credibility; practitioners who have done interesting things want a platform and an audience. A co-produced story can serve both — but only if the practitioner controls the narrative and the audience is other practitioners, not buyers.

Some activities create tension between the two sets of goals. These require honesty and care, not avoidance:

- **Advocacy pressure** — the organisation wants practitioners to recommend the technology; practitioners will recommend it if it is good and if they trust the relationship, but will resist if asked to perform advocacy they do not feel. The tension is resolved by earning advocacy, not requesting it.
- **Access as leverage** — roadmap sessions and early access are genuine benefits DevRel can offer Guardians; but if access is made explicitly conditional on advocacy, it poisons the relationship. Access should be given because practitioners' input is genuinely valued, not as payment for promotion.
- **Measurement** — the organisation needs to demonstrate DevRel's value; practitioners do not want to be reduced to metrics or to feel their relationship is being tracked for reporting purposes. The tension is managed by measuring ecosystem health signals rather than relationship transactions.
- **Commercial context** — a practitioner who is also a customer exists in two relationships simultaneously: the practitioner relationship (non-transactional, DevRel-mediated) and the commercial relationship (transactional, Sales/CS-mediated). Conflating them destroys the former. DevRel needs to be clear internally about which relationship it holds and protect the boundary.

But the value exchange can fail. In two directions. Double trouble.

**The organisation extracts without investing.** Knowledge flows from practitioners to the organisation (feedback, signal, advocacy) without a commensurate flow of access, recognition, or genuine hearing in return. Practitioners notice. The Guardians — the ones with the most to give and the most to lose — disengage first. The ecosystem becomes shallower. The signal dries up. The advocacy becomes hollow. What looks like a healthy user base is in fact a set of Beneficiaries who will move on as soon as a better option appears.

**The organisation invests without purpose.** Resources flow outward — events, content, education — without a coherent understanding of what the organisation needs from the ecosystem and how those investments connect to it. DevRel becomes an activity function: doing things that look like DevRel without a clear theory of how they change the graph. The investment is real; the return is unmeasurable; the function is defunded.

The healthy exchange is intentional in both directions. The organisation knows what it needs from the ecosystem and designs programmes that earn it — by genuinely serving the ecosystem's goals first. DevRel is the function that holds that intention, manages the interface, and keeps both sides of the exchange honest.
