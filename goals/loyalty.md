# Loyalty (strategic goal)

**program family:** [Involvement](../programs/3-involvement.md) **Driven by:**
Events, Open source and contribution, Guardians program

Loyalty is the organisational goal of ensuring that practitioners who have
adopted the technology develop a durable, active stake in the ecosystem — a
stake rooted in genuine relationships, genuine contribution, and genuine shared
experience, not in switching costs or vendor lock-in.

This is the goal that the
[Involvement program family](../programs/3-involvement.md) is primarily designed
to serve. Everything that deepens the edges between practitioners, between
practitioners and the organisation, and between practitioners and the technology
itself is a Loyalty investment.
## What loyalty actually means

Loyalty in the commercial sense — the practitioner who stays because they have
no alternative — is not the kind DevRel is pursuing and is not the kind that
generates a healthy ecosystem. A practitioner who stays out of inertia or
because switching costs are prohibitive is not a Guardian in waiting. They are a
retained user who has no particular reason to give back.

The Loyalty DevRel builds is relational: the practitioner who stays because the
ecosystem is genuinely valuable to them, because they have real relationships
within it, because they have invested in it and feel invested in return, because
it is not just a tool they use but a community they belong to.

The distinction has practical consequences. Relational loyalty:

- **Survives competitive pressure.** The practitioner who is genuinely embedded
  in an ecosystem will not abandon it for a competitor with slightly better
  pricing or a marginally improved feature. They have built relationships,
  contributed content, developed operational knowledge, and made investments
  that have no equivalent outside the ecosystem. The switching cost they feel is
  real, but it is relational rather than technical.
- **Generates Advocacy.** The practitioner whose loyalty is contractual does not
  recommend the technology to others. The practitioner whose loyalty is
  relational does — because the ecosystem has given them something worth
  sharing, and because they have a stake in the technology being well-regarded.
- **Feeds the value exchange.** The loyal practitioner contributes: they answer
  questions, flag issues, participate in feedback sessions, speak at events,
  write about their experience. Their continued participation is a direct
  contribution to the ecosystem's health.

Loyalty, in this sense, is not a retention metric. It is a relationship quality
metric. And it is the relationship quality that makes the retention sustainable.
## The relationship between loyalty and the practitioner journey

Loyalty is built across the later journey stages — from
[Acclimation](../journey/acclimation.md) through
[Application](../journey/application.md) toward
[Advocacy](../journey/advocacy.md) — but is most actively cultivated during
Application, when the practitioner is getting real value and is in the right
position to engage with the ecosystem more fully.

The practitioner who reaches Application without having any meaningful
connection to the broader ecosystem — who has used the technology in isolation,
without engaging with community, events, or feedback channels — is a user, not a
member. They have adopted without developing loyalty in the relational sense.
They are more vulnerable to competitive alternatives and less likely to become a
Guardian.

The Involvement programs are the mechanism for changing this: creating events,
contribution opportunities, and Guardian-level relationships that give the
Application-stage practitioner reasons to connect with the ecosystem and with
each other.
## What the organisation needs to do

**Create genuine reasons to engage, not gamified incentives.** Loyalty programs
that reduce practitioner engagement to points, badges, and leaderboards are
building the wrong kind of loyalty — the loyalty of a rewards member, not the
loyalty of a community member. The practitioner who participates in a hackathon
because it is genuinely valuable to them — because they learn something, build
something, meet someone — is more loyal after the event. The practitioner who
participates because of the prize is not more loyal. They are more aware of the
prize-giving mechanism.

This does not mean recognition is wrong — it means that recognition should
reflect genuine investment. The Guardian who receives meaningful, personalised
acknowledgement of a specific contribution is being recognised. The practitioner
who receives a batch-processed badge is being managed.

**Invest in practitioner-to-practitioner connections, not just
practitioner-to-vendor.** The Loyalty that is most durable is built on direct
relationships between practitioners — not on a bilateral relationship between
the practitioner and the organisation. A practitioner whose loyalty is mediated
entirely through their relationship with DevRel will disengage if that DevRel
practitioner leaves. A practitioner whose loyalty is built on direct friendships
with five other practitioners in the ecosystem is embedded in a way that no
individual departure can easily dissolve.

This is why the definition of Involvement matters: events where practitioners
speak to each other are categorically different from events where the vendor
speaks to practitioners. The former builds direct practitioner-to-practitioner
edges. The latter builds practitioner-to-vendor edges — valuable, but less
durable.

**Take contribution seriously.** The practitioner who contributes — whether
code, documentation, answers, or presence — is signalling a level of investment
that should be met with a commensurate organisational response. The contribution
process that is opaque, that leaves pull requests unreviewed for months, that
acknowledges contributions with automated responses, communicates that the
organisation does not actually want the contribution — it just wants to appear
to want it. This is one of the fastest ways to destroy loyalty in practitioners
who had the highest Guardian potential.

**Protect the non-transactional relationship.** The loyalty DevRel builds is at
risk whenever it is conflated with a commercial relationship. The practitioner
who is also a customer exists in two relationships — the practitioner
relationship (trust-based, non-transactional, DevRel-mediated) and the
commercial relationship (contractual, transactional, Sales/CS-mediated). When
these are conflated — when the DevRel relationship is used as a lever for
commercial objectives — the trust layer is damaged and often cannot be repaired.
## The tension with retention as a metric

Loyalty and retention are related but different. Retention is a lagging
indicator: the practitioner has not yet churned. Loyalty is a leading indicator:
the practitioner has reasons not to churn, is actively engaged, and would be
difficult to poach.

The organisation that measures Loyalty through retention rates is measuring the
wrong thing at the wrong frequency. A practitioner can be retained for a year
while their loyalty is steadily declining — they have not yet found a compelling
alternative, but they are not engaged, not contributing, and not advocating. The
moment a compelling alternative appears, they are gone.

Loyalty is better measured through engagement quality: the proportion of
practitioners who are active in community channels, the Guardian growth rate,
the return rate at events, the contribution recurrence rate. These are leading
indicators of whether the ecosystem is building real loyalty or just deferring
churn.
## DevRel's role

The primary practitioner instrument for the Loyalty goal is the
[Connected Correspondent](../practitioners/connected-correspondent.md). The CC's
relational instrument — the deep, non-transactional connections that make
practitioners feel genuinely known and valued — is what builds and maintains the
kind of loyalty that survives competitive pressure.

The [Expert Educator](../practitioners/expert-educator.md) contributes through
the content and learning opportunities that make continued engagement feel
worthwhile — the advanced education, the deep-dive sessions, the technical
community that gives practitioners reasons to keep showing up.

The [Reputable Resonator](../practitioners/reputable-resonator.md) contributes
through the presence that makes gatherings feel like they matter and the
Guardian recognition that signals to practitioners that their contribution to
the ecosystem elevates their standing in the field.

The [Involvement program family](../programs/3-involvement.md) is the structural
home of the Loyalty goal: Events, Open source and contribution, and the
Guardians program. These are the investments that build the relational edges on
which durable loyalty depends.
