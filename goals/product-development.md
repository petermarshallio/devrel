# Product development (strategic goal)

**Programme family:** [Innovation](../programs/innovation.md)
**Driven by:** Feedback, Roadmap sessions, Design partnerships

Product development is the organisational goal of using the ecosystem's intelligence — the operational experience, the friction points, the unmet needs, the architectural patterns — to build better product. It is DevRel's most strategically distinctive contribution to the business: knowledge of the ecosystem that no other function holds, surfaced in a form that product teams can act on.

This is the goal that the [Innovation programme family](../programs/innovation.md) is primarily designed to serve. Everything that moves genuine ecosystem intelligence from practitioners to product decision-makers is a Product development investment.

---

## What product development (as a DevRel goal) actually means

Product development as a strategic goal for DevRel is not about DevRel running product management — it is about DevRel giving product management something it cannot get through its own channels.

Product teams have access to:
- Structured customer interviews (curated, with agenda, with commercial context)
- Support tickets (the things that broke badly enough to file a formal complaint)
- Sales feedback (what customers say they want in order to buy or renew)
- Usage data (what practitioners actually do, without context or explanation)

What product teams do not have easy access to is:
- The unfiltered practitioner opinion: what they say when there is no commercial relationship at stake, no support SLA, no sales process
- The operational edge cases: the things that are working badly in ways too subtle to file a ticket about but significant enough to be a genuine problem
- The adjacent context: the architectural choices practitioners are making around the technology, the ecosystem dependencies forming, the integration patterns emerging before they become standard
- The practitioner-to-practitioner conversation: what is being said in community channels about the technology, what practitioners tell each other that they would never tell a vendor

This is what DevRel can supply. And it is genuinely differentiating intelligence — not because DevRel has access to better sources of information, but because the non-transactional, trust-based relationships that the [Connected Correspondent](../practitioners/connected-correspondent.md) builds are the mechanism through which unfiltered signal flows.

## The relationship between product development and the practitioner journey

Product development as a goal maps to the latest stages of the practitioner journey — [Application](../journey/application.md) and [Advocacy](../journey/advocacy.md). Practitioners who have reached Application have the depth of operational experience necessary to give genuinely useful product feedback. They have run the technology in real conditions, encountered its real limits, and formed real opinions. Their feedback is specific and credible in a way that early-stage feedback is not.

This is why the Product development goal is sequentially dependent on the Adoption and Loyalty goals. A practitioner who has not yet reached Application does not have the operational depth to contribute meaningfully to product direction. A practitioner who is at Application but not loyal — not embedded in the ecosystem — has little reason to invest the effort that meaningful product contribution requires.

The Guardian programme is the point at which these dependencies converge: the Guardian has completed the full journey, is actively contributing, and has earned the access and trust that makes genuine product partnership possible.

## What the organisation needs to do

**Create a genuine feedback loop, not a feedback theatre.** The most common failure mode for the Product development goal is building the infrastructure of feedback without building the substance. Roadmap sessions, feedback forums, and design partnerships that collect practitioner input but never visibly act on it are worse than nothing — they create an expectation of influence, fail to meet it, and damage the trust that made the feedback possible in the first place.

A genuine feedback loop requires:
- A mechanism for practitioners to provide structured, specific feedback
- A process for routing that feedback to the people who can act on it
- A response, at some meaningful interval, that tells the practitioner what was heard and what changed — or, if nothing changed, why

The closed-loop acknowledgement is the part most often neglected. Practitioners who are told "we have heard you" but never see the evidence do not believe it. Practitioners who are shown "we heard this, and here is how it influenced this decision" feel genuinely invested in the product's development.

**Distinguish types of feedback.** Not all ecosystem feedback is relevant to the same decisions. DevRel needs to be able to distinguish:

- **Friction feedback** — things that are harder than they should be; friction that adds cost to adoption without adding capability; a target for documentation, education, or product polish
- **Feature requests** — capabilities practitioners need that the product does not yet have; material for roadmap prioritisation
- **Architectural signals** — patterns in how practitioners are building; the integrations they are constructing; the use cases that are emerging before the product team anticipated them
- **Strategic intelligence** — the emerging competitive context; the technologies practitioners are comparing; the solutions they are building when this technology cannot yet serve their need

Each type routes to a different consumer: friction to the documentation and education team; feature requests to the product roadmap; architectural signals to product strategy; strategic intelligence to leadership. The DevRel practitioner who can surface and correctly route all four types is providing genuine strategic value.

**Protect the non-transactional relationship.** The Product development goal creates a risk that is worth naming explicitly. The practitioner who trusts the [Connected Correspondent](../practitioners/connected-correspondent.md) enough to say "honestly, the performance under this access pattern is broken and I cannot see any evidence it is being fixed" is trusting that the CC is not going to use that information to manage the commercial relationship. The moment that trust is violated — the moment the CC's feedback role becomes a mechanism for account management — the honest signal disappears. The practitioner learns to say the curated version.

This is why the CC's relationship with the practitioner must remain structurally separate from the commercial relationship. The CC who is also managing the account cannot be the person who gets the unfiltered version.

**Invest in product responsiveness.** The Product development goal is upstream of what the product team does with the signal. DevRel can supply the best ecosystem intelligence in the industry; if the product team does not have a mechanism for processing and acting on it, the investment is wasted. The organisation that wants DevRel to contribute to Product development needs to ensure that the product team has the bandwidth and the mandate to engage with what DevRel surfaces.

## The tension with product management

Product development as a DevRel goal can create tension with the product management function, which has its own sources of prioritisation input (commercial customers, market research, competitive analysis, engineering capacity). DevRel's ecosystem intelligence is one input among many, and DevRel practitioners sometimes feel that their signals are not weighted appropriately.

The resolution to this tension is not for DevRel to become product management — it is for DevRel to supply intelligence in a form that product management can integrate: specific, well-evidenced, with clear source (what kind of practitioner said this, in what context, with what operational experience), and distinguished by type (friction, feature request, architectural signal, strategic intelligence).

The DevRel practitioner who brings "practitioners are frustrated with X" without specificity, evidence, or context will not move the product roadmap. The one who brings "seven practitioners running this at scale have independently encountered Y, here are the specific conditions, here is what they tried, here is what they needed that does not yet exist" is giving product management something they cannot get elsewhere.

## DevRel's role

The primary practitioner instrument for the Product development goal is the [Connected Correspondent](../practitioners/connected-correspondent.md). The trust-based, non-transactional relationship is the mechanism through which unfiltered signal flows. Without it, the feedback that reaches the product team is the curated version — valuable, but not the same thing.

The [Expert Educator](../practitioners/expert-educator.md) contributes through technical synthesis: translating practitioner experience into specific, actionable product intelligence rather than general expressions of frustration. The EE who can take a practitioner's operational complaint and articulate the precise technical condition under which it manifests is making the feedback significantly more useful.

The [Innovation programme family](../programs/innovation.md) is the structural home of the Product development goal: Feedback programmes, Roadmap sessions, and Design partnerships. These are the investments that build the intelligence infrastructure that makes the Product development goal achievable.
