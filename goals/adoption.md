# Adoption (strategic goal)

**Programme family:** [Information](../programs/information.md)
**Driven by:** Education, Documentation, Tutoring

Adoption is the organisational goal of moving practitioners from evaluation to active, sustained use of the technology. It is not enough that practitioners have heard of the technology, or that they have started an integration and abandoned it. Adoption — in the strategic sense — means a practitioner is building real things, running them in real environments, and deriving real value.

This is the goal that the [Information programme family](../programs/information.md) is primarily designed to serve. Everything that reduces the cognitive cost of getting started, getting unstuck, and getting to production is an Adoption investment.

---

## What adoption actually means

The word "adoption" gets used loosely. In product teams it often means any activation event — a sign-up, a first API call, a completed tutorial. In marketing it might mean reaching a certain threshold of registered users. In DevRel, adoption has a more specific meaning: the practitioner is using the technology to solve a real problem they actually have, in a real environment that matters to them.

The distinction is important because shallow adoption and deep adoption have very different downstream consequences.

**Shallow adoption** is fragile. The practitioner who signed up, completed a tutorial, and never returned is a data point in the activation funnel but is not contributing to ecosystem health. They will not advocate. They will not contribute. They may appear in usage statistics while generating no genuine value for the ecosystem or for themselves.

**Deep adoption** is durable. The practitioner who has built something real with the technology — who has solved a problem they genuinely had, who has gone through [Acclimation](../journey/acclimation.md) and come out the other side — has a qualitatively different relationship with the technology. They have skin in the game. Their operational experience is real. Their opinion is credible. They are a candidate for Guardianship.

DevRel's goal is deep adoption. The metrics that matter are not activation counts but behavioural indicators: time to first meaningful deployment, practitioner-reported confidence at key stages, and downstream signals of continued use and growing engagement.

## The relationship between adoption and the practitioner journey

Adoption as a strategic goal maps to specific stages in the [practitioner journey](../programs/README.md): most directly to [Assessment](../journey/assessment.md) (the decision to adopt), [Adoption](../journey/adoption.md) (the building phase), and [Acclimation](../journey/acclimation.md) (the production bedding-in period).

Each of these stages has a characteristic failure mode that prevents the Adoption goal from being reached:

- **Assessment** fails when practitioners cannot determine whether the technology fits their use case, or when the getting-started experience does not match the assessment claims
- **Adoption** fails when the documentation does not cover real-world complexity, when practitioners get stuck without access to unblocking resources, or when the cognitive load of building is higher than necessary
- **Acclimation** fails when post-production support is absent, when operational documentation lags the product, or when practitioners who encounter production problems feel unsupported

The Adoption strategic goal requires investment across all three of these journey stages, not just the Assessment funnel. Organisations that invest heavily in acquiring practitioners who then fail at Adoption or Acclimation are spending resources to produce churn — which is worse than not acquiring them, because the churned practitioner now has a negative story to tell.

## What the organisation needs to do

**Reduce the cost of the first meaningful deployment.** Every unnecessary step between a practitioner's decision to adopt and their first successful production deployment is a barrier to the Adoption goal. This is not about making things artificially simple — it is about making the essential path clear, well-documented, and free of avoidable obstacles.

**Invest in operational documentation, not just getting-started content.** The Adoption goal is not achieved when the practitioner completes a tutorial — it is achieved when they are running something in production that matters to them. The documentation that covers the operational reality of the technology, not just the happy path, is what determines whether practitioners reach that point.

**Maintain the feedback loop.** The practitioners who are actively adopting are the best source of signal about what is making adoption hard. Building mechanisms to capture and route that signal — to the documentation team, to the product team, to the education team — is how the organisation continuously improves the adoption experience rather than allowing adoption friction to compound.

**Measure outcomes, not activities.** The number of tutorials published, the number of workshops run, and the number of office hours sessions held are activity metrics. They are necessary but not sufficient. The Adoption goal is measured by outcomes: practitioners who successfully reached production, who report confidence in what they built, who stayed engaged beyond the initial activation.

## How adoption relates to the other strategic goals

Adoption does not exist in isolation from the other three strategic goals. It is the foundation on which they depend:

- **[Market awareness](market-awareness.md)** creates the conditions for Adoption to be possible — practitioners who have never heard of the technology cannot adopt it
- **[Loyalty](loyalty.md)** is what deep adoption becomes over time — the practitioner who has invested in building something real has a stake in the technology's success
- **[Product development](product-development.md)** is partly powered by the signal generated by practitioners who are actively adopting — the friction they encounter, the features they need, the patterns they discover

The organisation that achieves Adoption but neglects Loyalty will find itself perpetually re-acquiring practitioners who do not stay. The one that achieves Loyalty without ongoing Adoption growth has a stable but shrinking ecosystem. Adoption is the engine; the other goals are what make the engine worth running.

## DevRel's role

The primary practitioner instrument for the Adoption goal is the [Expert Educator](../practitioners/expert-educator.md). The EE's capability — reducing cognitive load through good explanation, building curriculum that moves practitioners through the learning curve efficiently, maintaining documentation that covers the real-world experience — is the core of what drives Adoption.

The [Connected Correspondent](../practitioners/connected-correspondent.md) contributes through the tutoring and community relationships that provide the human layer of support: the practitioner who is stuck at 11pm and finds a responsive community, or who can book an office hours session with someone who will actually help them, has a qualitatively better adoption experience.

The [Information programme family](../programs/information.md) is the structural home of the Adoption goal: Education, Documentation, and Tutoring. These are the investments that move the needle on deep adoption.
