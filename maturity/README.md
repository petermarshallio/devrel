# Maturity

Ecosystem maturity is not about how enthusiastic practitioners are. It is about
how operationally capable and collaboratively resilient the ecosystem is.

A highly engaged ecosystem can still be entirely dependent on the vendor to
answer questions, route connections, and initiate everything. An ecosystem that
has reached maturity requires none of that. Its practitioners help each other.
Its Guardians carry its knowledge wherever they go. Its edges are dense enough
that losing any single node — including DevRel itself — does not collapse the
network.

The difference is not measured in follower counts or event attendance. It is
measured in graph topology.

## The five levels

Ecosystem maturity progresses through five stages. Each describes a different
graph structure — a different pattern of how nodes relate to each other and to
the organisation.
### Level 1 — Isolated

**Structure:** Disconnected nodes. Few or no edges between practitioners.

Practitioners exist individually. They may be using the technology, but they are
not in relationship with each other or with the organisation. There is no
community in any meaningful sense — just separate individuals with a shared
tool.

DevRel, at this stage, is the only visible node. All value flows from the
organisation outward. Practitioners are Beneficiaries only — there is no
mechanism for them to become Donors.

**What DevRel focuses on:** Outreach. Identification. Making the ecosystem
legible. Building the earliest connections — between the organisation and
individual practitioners, and where possible, between practitioners themselves.

**What signals indicate this level:**

- Near-zero practitioner-to-practitioner interaction in public channels
- All questions go to official support or are abandoned unanswered
- No user-generated content, tutorials, or extensions in the wild
- No practitioners citing each other's work
### Level 2 — Dependent

**Structure:** Star topology. All edges pass through DevRel or the vendor.

Practitioners now know where to go to get help, and they go there. The community
has a centre, but DevRel is the centre. Every connection is mediated. Every
answer comes from the organisation. The ecosystem has energy, but it is energy
directed inward, not outward.

This is the most common state for growing developer ecosystems, and it is
frequently mistaken for success. Engagement is high. The community appears
active. But the activity is entirely dependent on DevRel sustaining it. If
DevRel stops answering questions, the questions go unanswered. The ecosystem has
no self-healing capability.

**What DevRel focuses on:** Building capability, not just answering questions.
Every interaction at this stage should be reducing future dependency: better
documentation, better self-service, and actively identifying practitioners who
might answer questions as well as ask them.

**What signals indicate this level:**

- High question volume, low peer-to-peer answer rate
- DevRel or vendor staff are the primary contributors in community channels
- Repeat questions on the same topics (documentation is not doing its job)
- Practitioners who ask tend to thank the organisation, not each other
### Level 3 — Contributing

**Structure:** Distributed edges beginning to form. Some nodes connect directly
to each other.

Guardians are beginning to emerge. A small number of practitioners are answering
questions unprompted, creating content, contributing back. The first direct
practitioner-to-practitioner edges appear in the graph. The ecosystem is no
longer entirely dependent on DevRel as the router.

This is the first stage where the ecosystem shows signs of self-sustaining
capacity. It is fragile — the emerging Guardians need recognition and support,
or they will stop contributing and the ecosystem will regress. But the
structural foundation is there.

**What DevRel focuses on:** Guardian identification and cultivation. This is
where the [Connected Correspondent](../practitioners/connected-correspondent.md)
becomes critical. The first Guardians need to feel that their contribution is
seen, valued, and consequential — that they are part of something real, not just
volunteering for a vendor.

**What signals indicate this level:**

- A small cohort of practitioners answering questions without prompting
- User-generated content (tutorials, integrations, extensions) appearing
  organically
- Practitioners citing each other's work alongside the official documentation
- Guardian ratio (answers given vs. questions asked) above 1 for at least a
  handful of practitioners
### Level 4 — Participating

**Structure:** Rich graph. Many direct connections. DevRel as one node among
many, not the hub.

The ecosystem has enough internal connectivity that practitioners can get help,
find collaborators, and navigate the technology without routing everything
through DevRel. Guardians are numerous and active. The documentation is
supplemented by a substantial body of practitioner-created content. Events
happen that DevRel did not organise.

This is the stage most DevRel functions aim for. The ecosystem is visibly
healthy, and the health is distributed rather than centralised. DevRel's role
shifts: less question-answering and outreach, more stewardship — maintaining the
conditions that allow the ecosystem to keep operating at this level.

**What DevRel focuses on:** Sustaining the conditions. Guardian retention.
Ensuring that the practitioners who are load-bearing in the graph continue to
have what they need (access, recognition, influence). Watching for and
addressing pockets of isolation where the Level 1 or 2 structure persists
despite the overall ecosystem health.

**What signals indicate this level:**

- Practitioner-to-practitioner answer rate exceeds DevRel's answer rate
- Regular community-initiated events, working groups, or projects
- Multiple Guardian-tier practitioners across different geographies and use
  cases
- New practitioners report finding answers in practitioner-created content, not
  just official documentation
### Level 5 — Self-organising

**Structure:** Resilient mesh. No single point of failure. The ecosystem
operates independently of DevRel routing.

The ecosystem has reached a state where it can sustain itself. It would not
collapse if DevRel disappeared tomorrow. Practitioners help each other without
prompting. Guardians recruit new contributors. The knowledge, the confidence,
and the relationships are distributed across the graph rather than held
centrally.

DevRel at this stage is not redundant — but its role is fundamentally different.
It is no longer the engine of the ecosystem. It is the observer, the calibrator,
the occasional catalyst. It surfaces Intel. It spots the early warning signs of
regression. It ensures that the ecosystem's health remains legible to the
organisation.

**What DevRel focuses on:** Observability and calibration. The risk at Level 5
is not stagnation — it is invisible regression. The graph can begin to lose
edges without anyone noticing until the damage is significant. DevRel's job is
to instrument the signals that show whether the ecosystem is maintaining its
structure.

**What signals indicate this level:**

- Self-initiated contribution rates that do not depend on DevRel prompting
- Guardian program operating with significant practitioner-led governance
- New practitioner onboarding primarily through peer channels, not vendor
  outreach
- Ecosystem-originated forks, extensions, or projects that the organisation did
  not commission
## Measuring maturity

Ecosystem maturity is measured through signals, not scores. No single metric
determines maturity level — it is the pattern of signals across the graph that
reveals where the ecosystem sits.

The Intel layer that runs beneath all four [program families](../programs.md) is
the instrumentation mechanism. Signals worth tracking:

### Graph connectivity

- Practitioner-to-practitioner interaction rate in community channels
- Ratio of questions answered by practitioners vs. answered by vendor/DevRel
- Number of practitioners above the Guardian threshold (giving more than they
  take)

### Distributed capability

- Volume and quality of practitioner-created content (tutorials, integrations,
  extensions)
- New practitioner onboarding routes (what percentage find peers first vs.
  vendor first)
- Geographic and use-case diversity of Guardian-tier practitioners

### Self-healing capacity

- Response time to unanswered questions when DevRel does not intervene
- Recovery rate after DevRel absence (events, holidays, team changes)
- Proportion of the ecosystem's knowledge held in practitioner-created versus
  vendor-created resources

### Ecosystem health trajectory

- Guardian growth rate over time
- Contributor recurrence rate (first-time contributors who contribute again)
- Churn among Guardian-tier practitioners (a leading indicator of underlying
  ecosystem problems)
## What DevRel's role looks like at each stage

| Level | Primary DevRel activity | Primary program family |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Isolated** | Outreach; discovery; first-connection building | Information |
| **Dependent** | Capability transfer; documentation; reducing dependency | Information + Inspiration |
| **Contributing** | Guardian cultivation; Involvement program activation | Involvement |
| **Participating** | Stewardship; Guardian retention; ecosystem sensing | Involvement + Innovation |
| **Self-organising** | Observability; calibration; strategic intervention | Innovation + Intel |

The paradox of DevRel maturity is that success looks like becoming less
necessary as the central actor. The function that has worked well enough has
built an ecosystem that no longer needs it to route every connection. That is
not failure — it is the goal. The DevRel function that has reached this is not
less valuable; it is more strategically positioned, because it holds the
observability layer that no other function can replicate.
