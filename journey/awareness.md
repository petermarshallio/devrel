# Awareness

**The practitioner's question:** What is this, and is it relevant to me?

Awareness is the stage at which a practitioner moves from a vague signal — someone mentioned this, it came up twice last week — to a functional understanding of what a technology is, what problems it solves, and whether those problems are their problems. It is the formation of a mental model.

The practitioner is not yet evaluating whether to use the technology. They are deciding whether it belongs in their consideration set at all — whether it is worth the effort of a proper Assessment.

---

## What is happening for the practitioner

The practitioner at the Awareness stage is doing active sense-making. They have decided, on the basis of an [Attraction](attraction.md) signal, that this is worth looking into. Now they are asking the foundational questions: what category does this belong to? What does it replace or complement? Who is using it, and for what? How does it fit — or not fit — into the architecture I already run?

This is a pattern-matching exercise against their existing knowledge and their current problem space. The practitioner who already has a clear picture of their needs and a well-developed mental model of the solution space will move through Awareness quickly — the new technology either fits a known slot or it does not. The practitioner who is less certain of their own problem, or who is entering a new domain, needs more from the Awareness stage.

What both need is clarity. Not depth — depth comes later, at [Assessment](assessment.md) and [Adoption](adoption.md). At Awareness, the practitioner needs to understand:

- What the technology is and is not
- What problems it is designed to solve
- What it is not designed to solve (equally important)
- Whether any of those problems are their problems

The failure mode at Awareness is not ignorance — it is confusion. A practitioner who has absorbed a lot of content about a technology but still cannot articulate what it is for has not achieved Awareness. They have been exposed to information without forming a model.

---

## What makes Awareness succeed

**Category clarity before feature depth.** Practitioners build mental models by categorising first, then differentiating. The content that succeeds at Awareness tells the practitioner what bucket to put the technology in before explaining what makes it different from everything else in that bucket. "This is a stream processing engine" precedes "here is what makes it faster than its competitors."

**Honest scoping.** Technologies that claim to do everything are harder to develop Awareness of than technologies with clear, acknowledged scope. The practitioner needs to understand not just what the technology does but what it does not do — so they can accurately assess whether it is relevant to their situation. Overclaiming at Awareness creates a credibility deficit that compounds through every later stage.

**Practitioner-facing language.** Technical practitioners are fluent in the language of their domain and impatient with abstraction that hides specificity. Awareness content that speaks in practitioner terms — architecture patterns, use cases, the specific problems that arise in production — builds mental models more effectively than content written for a general audience.

**Multiple formats for different learning styles.** Some practitioners build Awareness through a well-structured overview article. Others need a five-minute video. Others will read a comparison table. The investment in Awareness content across formats is not redundancy — it is meeting practitioners at their own entry point.

**Accessible without registration.** Practitioners who encounter friction at the moment of Awareness — forms to fill in, emails to verify, sales conversations to navigate — will usually route around it or abandon the investigation. Awareness content needs to be freely discoverable and immediately accessible.

---

## What makes Awareness fail

**Feature-first positioning.** Content that leads with capabilities and feature lists before establishing what the technology is for leaves practitioners unable to anchor the information. Without a clear mental model of the category, feature depth is noise.

**Jargon without grounding.** Every technical domain has internal language that is precise and efficient for practitioners already inside it. Using that language at the Awareness stage with practitioners who are encountering the technology for the first time creates a barrier rather than a bridge.

**Conflation with marketing.** Practitioners at the Awareness stage are doing genuine sense-making, not consuming brand content. Material that reads as promotional — that emphasises positioning over clarity — signals that the organisation is more interested in managing the practitioner's perception than in helping them understand. That signal damages trust precisely when trust is being formed for the first time.

**Gaps between what is claimed and what is easily discovered.** If the Awareness content says the technology is easy to get started with, but the documentation is sparse or the getting-started experience is broken, the practitioner's Awareness becomes actively negative — they have now formed an accurate model, and it is unflattering.

## DevRel's role at Awareness

The [Expert Educator](../practitioners/expert-educator.md) is the natural practitioner at this stage. The EE's instrument — clarity, structure, the ability to make complex things accessible — is exactly what Awareness content requires. The EE who can write the overview article that a practitioner reads once and walks away from with a clear mental model is doing work that compounds across every subsequent stage of the journey.

The [Reputable Resonator](../practitioners/reputable-resonator.md) also contributes — particularly in contexts where the practitioner encounters the technology through a talk or piece of content that establishes both what it is and why it matters. The RR's skill at narrative and conviction is valuable at Awareness because it creates not just understanding but motivation to continue.

Awareness content lives primarily in the [Inspiration programme family](../programs/inspiration.md) (conference talks, overview content, social presence) and the early layers of the [Information programme family](../programs/information.md) (conceptual documentation, overview guides). The dividing line is approximately: Inspiration creates the signal that attracts attention; Information provides the model that satisfies it.

The goal at Awareness is a practitioner who can, without difficulty, explain to a colleague what this technology is, what it does, and whether it is worth their colleague's time to investigate further. That practitioner has genuine Awareness. The practitioner who cannot do that has been exposed to content but has not arrived at understanding.
