# Advocacy

**The practitioner's question:** I want others to know about this. I want to
contribute.

Advocacy is the stage at which the practitioner begins giving back to the
ecosystem that gave to them. They write the article. They answer the question in
the community channel. They give the talk at the meetup. They introduce the
technology to a colleague. They file the detailed bug report, contribute the
fix, or build the integration that others will later depend on.

Advocacy is not a marketing outcome. It is the natural expression of a
practitioner who has received genuine value, has developed genuine expertise,
and has found an ecosystem worth contributing to. DevRel does not create
advocacy — it creates the conditions under which advocacy is the natural next
step.
## What is happening for the practitioner

The practitioner at Advocacy has completed the full journey from
[Attraction](attraction.md) through [Application](application.md). They have
operational experience, real opinions, and a body of knowledge that other
practitioners earlier in the journey do not yet have. The thing that makes
Advocacy feel natural — rather than effortful — is the realisation that their
knowledge is genuinely useful to others.

That realisation does not always arise spontaneously. Sometimes it comes from a
moment: a colleague asks them a question they can answer with confidence, and
they realise the answer is not obvious. Sometimes it comes from an encounter:
they read a piece of content about the technology that is incomplete or
misleading, and they feel the impulse to correct the record. Sometimes it comes
from an invitation: someone asks them to speak, or to contribute, or to help,
and they discover in the doing of it that they had more to give than they
thought.

Advocacy takes many forms. Not every Advocate gives keynotes. The practitioner
who answers questions in the community channel — consistently, accurately,
helpfully — is an Advocate. The practitioner who writes the blog post their
colleague reads before making their Assessment decision is an Advocate. The
practitioner who contributes the example code that another practitioner will
build on during Adoption is an Advocate. The practitioner who tells their
manager "you should look at this" is an Advocate.

The common thread is not visibility or scale. It is the direction of value: the
Advocate is giving rather than only receiving. They have crossed the line —
often without noticing — from Beneficiary to Donor, or from Donor to Guardian.
## The Guardian distinction

Advocacy and Guardian status are related but not identical. Every Guardian is an
Advocate, but not every Advocate is a Guardian.

A Guardian is a practitioner who has become structurally load-bearing in the
ecosystem — whose participation creates edges that others depend on, whose
departure would leave gaps that others would feel. Guardians are not just
contributing; they are the scaffolding of the ecosystem's resilience.

The Advocate who is becoming a Guardian does so gradually. They contribute once,
and it is well-received. They contribute again. Others begin to rely on their
answers. Their name becomes recognisable in community channels. program
committees notice them. Other practitioners start citing their work. At some
point — not at a specific moment but through accumulation — they have become
load-bearing.

DevRel's role is to notice this transition and respond to it: to identify the
Advocate who is becoming a Guardian, to invest in that relationship, and to
create the conditions (access, recognition, genuine influence) under which the
Guardian's investment in the ecosystem continues to be worthwhile.
## What makes Advocacy succeed

**Genuine conviction, not manufactured enthusiasm.** The most valuable Advocacy
is the practitioner speaking honestly from their own experience — including its
difficult parts. The practitioner who says "it took me three weeks to get the
performance I needed in production, but I have learned enough now that I could
help someone else avoid the same mistakes" is more credible than the
practitioner who says the product is perfect. Authentic Advocacy, with its
honest acknowledgement of difficulty, is trusted in a way that promotional
advocacy is not.

**A platform that matches the Advocate's style.** Not every Advocate wants to
give a keynote. Not every Advocate writes well. Not every Advocate is a
networker. The ecosystem that only has room for one kind of Advocacy — the
visible, conference-speaking variety — systematically excludes practitioners who
have real value to give through other modes: written deep-dives, detailed
community answers, code contributions, behind-the-scenes mentoring. Expanding
the definition of what counts as Advocacy, and making all of its forms visible
and valued, increases the number of practitioners who can find their path into
contribution.

**The experience of having their contribution received well.** The first
contribution a practitioner makes is the most important one. If the community
response to their first community answer is positive, they will answer again. If
their first conference talk proposal is accepted and the experience is good,
they will propose again. If their first code contribution is reviewed
thoughtfully and merged, they will contribute again. If any of these first
experiences is poor — a dismissive response, a proposal rejection with no
feedback, a contribution ignored for months — the practitioner often does not
try again. The ecosystem's quality of response to first-time contributors is one
of its most important health indicators.

**Recognition that is meaningful, not performative.** Practitioners can tell the
difference between recognition that is genuine — that reflects a real
understanding of what they contributed — and recognition that is perfunctory.
The Guardian program participant who receives a batch-processed email thanking
them for being a "valued community member" has had a different experience from
the Guardian whose specific contribution was noticed and acknowledged by someone
who clearly read it. At scale, personalisation is hard. But the investment in
genuine recognition compounds in a way that batch processing does not.

**Access that is real.** The Advocate who has been invited into a Guardian
program and given "access to the product roadmap" needs to experience that
access as meaningful. If the roadmap sessions are briefings rather than
conversations — if the practitioner's questions receive non-answers, if the
feedback they provide never visibly influences anything — the promise of access
becomes a liability rather than an asset. Real access means the product team
genuinely engaging with what the Guardian has to say.
## What makes Advocacy fail

**Being instrumentalised.** Practitioners who feel that they are being used —
that their Advocacy is valued primarily for its marketing impact rather than for
what it represents about the ecosystem's health — will pull back. This is one of
the subtler forms of ecosystem damage, because it often happens to the
practitioners with the highest Guardian potential. The CC who maintains
genuinely non-transactional relationships is the primary guard against this:
they are the person most likely to notice when a practitioner's engagement is
becoming more reluctant and to understand why.

**Advocacy without infrastructure.** The practitioner who wants to give a talk
but does not know how to get onto a conference program, who wants to write but
has no platform or audience, who wants to contribute code but finds the
contribution process opaque — will not advocate. The absence of visible,
accessible pathways into Advocacy acts as a filter that excludes the
practitioners who have the most to give but the least access to the networks
that make contribution easy.

**The ecosystem being unworthy of advocacy.** Practitioners do not Advocate for
technologies they do not genuinely believe in. The practitioner who has
encountered significant product limitations during [Acclimation](acclimation.md)
and Application — who has been frustrated by roadmap stagnation, poor
operational documentation, or unresolved known issues — will not become a
credible Advocate regardless of how well the Guardian program is run. Advocacy
is downstream of product quality and ecosystem health. DevRel can create the
conditions for Advocacy; it cannot manufacture Advocacy where the conditions do
not exist.
## DevRel's role at Advocacy

Advocacy is where all three practitioner instruments converge.

The [Connected Correspondent](../practitioners/connected-correspondent.md)
identifies and cultivates the Advocates who are becoming Guardians — maintaining
the relationships that make Advocacy feel supported and recognised. The CC is
the person who notices the first-time contributor and reaches out to say "I saw
what you did there — would you like to do more?"

The [Reputable Resonator](../practitioners/reputable-resonator.md) creates the
platforms that give Advocates visibility and amplification — the conference
program that includes practitioners, not just staff; the content series that
features real user stories; the endorsement that tells program committees and
audiences that this practitioner is worth listening to.

The [Expert Educator](../practitioners/expert-educator.md) supports Advocates in
refining and communicating their technical knowledge — the review that makes a
community answer more accurate, the feedback that makes a conference proposal
stronger, the co-authorship that gives a practitioner's first blog post the
reach it deserves.

The formal program homes for Advocacy are the
[Involvement family](../programs/3-involvement.md) (Guardian program, events,
open source contribution) and the
[Inspiration family](../programs/1-inspiration.md) (storytelling, content,
speaker development). But Advocacy is not primarily managed — it is cultivated.
The ecosystem that has earned it will generate it. The ecosystem that has not
cannot program its way to it.
